# Problem Validation Brief

> **Before we talk about your solution, technology, or platform — let's talk about the problem.**
>
> The goal of this document is to force a rigorous examination of *the problem you want to solve*, not the product you want to build. The best startups (Uber, Airbnb, Instagram, Stripe) didn't start with technology — they started with a specific, painful, observable problem that real people had. If we can't articulate the problem clearly here, no amount of clever engineering will save the project later.
>
> Please fill in every section. If a section is hard to answer, that's not a failure — it's a signal that more research or thinking is needed *before* a single line of code is written. Vague answers ("people want better X") are red flags. Specific answers ("freelance designers in Sydney spend ~4 hours every Friday chasing late invoices") are green flags.
>
> **Do not describe the solution anywhere in sections 1–7.** Section 8 is the only place where you may begin to hint at a direction.

## 1. The Problem in One Sentence

> *Write a single sentence describing the problem — not the solution, not the market, not the technology. Just the pain. A good test: if you replaced this sentence with a description of your product, would it still make sense? If yes, you've described the solution, not the problem. Re-write it. Example of a bad answer: "There's no good app for restaurant bookings." Example of a good answer: "Diners trying to book popular restaurants for Friday night can't see real-time availability and end up calling 4–5 places before finding a table."*

## 2. Who Specifically Has This Problem?

> *Describe the person who suffers from this problem. Be specific — age range, occupation, life situation, what their week looks like, what tools they currently use. "Everyone" is not an answer. "Small businesses" is not an answer. "Owner-operators of independent cafés in Australian capital cities with 1–5 staff" is an answer. The narrower and more vivid your description, the better. If you can name three real people who fit this description, even better — list them.*

## 3. How Did You Discover This Problem?

> *Did you experience it yourself? Did you watch someone struggle with it? Did you read about it? Did someone complain to you? The origin matters because problems you've personally lived with — or watched up close — tend to be real. Problems you imagined from your desk tend to be made up. If you haven't personally encountered this problem, explain what gives you unique insight into people who have.*

## 4. Evidence That the Problem Is Real

> *List concrete evidence — not opinions, not "I think", not "obviously people want this". Evidence looks like: conversations with at least 5–10 potential customers (with quotes), forum posts/Reddit threads/Twitter complaints, existing workarounds people use (spreadsheets, WhatsApp groups, paper notebooks), money already being spent on inferior solutions, time being wasted. If you haven't talked to potential customers yet, the honest answer is "I haven't validated this yet" — and that should be the next step before anything else.*

[Your answer here]

## 5. How Painful Is It? (Painkiller vs. Vitamin)

> *Is this problem something people actively suffer from and would pay to make go away (a painkiller)? Or is it a "nice to have" that would be cool if it existed (a vitamin)? Painkillers build companies; vitamins struggle. Quantify the pain if you can: how much time does it cost per week? How much money? How much frustration? Does it cause people to lose customers, miss deadlines, lose sleep? If the honest answer is "it's a mild annoyance", say so — that's important information.*

[Your answer here]

## 6. What Do People Do Today to Cope?

> *Nobody is sitting around waiting for your product. They're already solving this problem somehow — badly. Maybe with spreadsheets. Maybe by hiring an assistant. Maybe by just suffering. Maybe with a competitor's product they hate. Describe the current workaround in detail. The gap between the current workaround and an ideal solution is roughly the size of your opportunity. If the current workaround is "good enough", there's no business here.*

[Your answer here]

## 7. Why Hasn't This Been Solved Already?

> *This is the hardest and most important question. If the problem is real and painful, why doesn't a solution exist? Possible honest answers: (a) it has been solved, but the existing solutions are bad — explain why; (b) the problem only recently emerged due to some change in technology, regulation, or behaviour; (c) solving it requires an unusual combination of skills or insight; (d) the market was too small until recently. Possible dishonest answers: "no one else has thought of it" (almost never true), "everyone else is incompetent" (almost never true). If you can't give a credible answer here, the problem may not actually be a problem — or you may not understand it well enough yet.*

## 8. Imagined Solution (Briefly)

> *Now, and only now, you may describe the solution you have in mind — but keep it to a paragraph. No tech stack, no features list, no architecture diagrams. Just: "I imagine something like X that lets person Y do Z." We're checking that the solution maps cleanly onto the problem described above. If the solution requires a five-paragraph explanation, it's probably too complex for a first version.*

## 9. The "Simplest Possible Version" Test

> *What is the absolute minimum version of this solution that would still meaningfully reduce the pain for at least one real customer? Not the impressive version. Not the version with AI and a mobile app and a dashboard. The embarrassingly simple version. Instagram launched with filters and sharing — nothing else. Airbnb launched as a website with photos of three air mattresses. If you can't describe a tiny first version, the project will sprawl.*

[Your answer here]

## 10. How Will We Know We're Wrong?

> *What evidence, if we saw it in the first 3–6 months, would tell us to stop or pivot? "People won't use it" is too vague. Specific signals: "Fewer than X of the first Y users return after a week", "Customer interviews reveal they actually prefer their current workaround", "The problem turns out to affect a much smaller group than expected". Defining failure conditions upfront prevents the trap of pouring more money and time into something that isn't working because we're emotionally invested.*

[Your answer here]

## 11. Open Questions and Unknowns

> *List the things you genuinely don't know yet. Honesty here is a strength, not a weakness. "I don't know how much customers would pay" or "I don't know if this problem exists outside of my own city" are valuable admissions because they tell us what to investigate next. A document with no open questions is usually a document that hasn't been thought about hard enough.*

## 12. Next Step You'd Take Before Building Anything

> *If we agreed today that the next step was *not* to start building, what would you do instead? Ideal answers involve talking to more potential customers, running a landing page test, building a no-code prototype, or shadowing someone with the problem for a day. If the only answer you can think of is "start coding", we're not ready yet.*
